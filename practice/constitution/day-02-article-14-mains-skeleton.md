## Study Label

## Question

Explain Article 14 and the doctrine of reasonable classification under the Constitution of India.

## Learner Skeleton

### 1. Introduction

> Article 14 provides Equality before law and equal protection of laws.

### 2. Equality before law

> Under the Article 14, Equality before law is a negative concept, it means that law is equal for any person, and law is supreme, no man is above law.

### 3. Equal protection of laws

> The Equal protection of laws is a positive concept. It means the state shall not distinguish any person from the protection of the law, and every person has a right to be protected by law.

### 4. Reasonable classification

> It means, the object of the law shall be based on the Intelligible Differentia and Rational Nexus.

### 5. Two tests

> Intelligible Differentia and Rational Nexus are the two tests of Article 14.

### 6. Arbitrariness

> The state shall now distinguish, bias, arbitrary against any person.

### 7. Case laws

> Currently not aware of any case law by memory, although as per bare act K. Thimmappa vs. Chairman, Central Board of Directors, SBI A.I.R. 2001 S.C. 467.

### 8. Conclusion

> Article 14 states that no person shall be denied the equality before law, and shall be protect equally by law. The equality allows the reasonable classification based on two tests i.e. Intelligible Differentia meaning there should be a clear distinguishment for two 2 groups of people and Rational Nexus, meaning the object of the law shall have a rational point. This article also has the concept of Arbitrariness, which explains any state shall not be arbitrary, bias, partial, and distinguish for no appropriate reason or explanation.

## Feedback

The skeleton is structurally correct and covers all required headings. The learner has understood the main Article 14 concepts. However, some exam-language refinement is required.

## Corrections and Refinements

### Introduction

Good but too short. Add that Article 14 applies to **any person** and operates within the territory of India.

Refined version:

> Article 14 guarantees equality before law and equal protection of laws to any person within the territory of India. It is a general equality provision and prohibits unreasonable classification and arbitrary State action.

### Equality before law

Correct. Refine grammar and legal phrasing.

Refined version:

> Equality before law is a negative concept. It means that no person is above law, there is no special privilege in favour of any person, and every person is equally subject to the ordinary law of the land.

### Equal protection of laws

Partly correct. Avoid saying the State shall not distinguish any person at all. Article 14 permits reasonable classification. Say similarly situated persons must be treated alike.

Refined version:

> Equal protection of laws is a positive concept. It means that persons similarly situated must be treated alike and must receive equal protection through law. It permits reasonable classification but prohibits class legislation.

### Reasonable classification

Needs correction. Reasonable classification does not mean the object of the law is based on intelligible differentia and rational nexus. Rather, the **classification** must be based on intelligible differentia and that differentia must have rational nexus with the object of the law.

Refined version:

> Reasonable classification means that the State may classify persons or things for legal treatment, provided the classification is not arbitrary and satisfies the two-fold test of intelligible differentia and rational nexus.

### Two tests

Correct but incomplete. Add definitions.

Refined version:

> The two tests are: (i) the classification must be founded on an intelligible differentia which distinguishes persons or things grouped together from those left out; and (ii) the differentia must have a rational nexus with the object sought to be achieved by the law.

### Arbitrariness

Correct idea but wording needs correction. The phrase should be “shall not act arbitrarily”, not “shall now distinguish”.

Refined version:

> Article 14 also prohibits arbitrary State action. State action must not be unfair, irrational, biased, unguided, or without objective criteria. Arbitrariness is antithetical to equality.

### Case laws

The learner did not remember the core case laws. For Article 14 reasonable classification and arbitrariness, the following should be memorised first:

- Budhan Choudhry v. State of Bihar — two-fold test of reasonable classification.
- Ram Krishna Dalmia v. Justice S.R. Tendolkar — principles of reasonable classification.
- E.P. Royappa v. State of Tamil Nadu — equality is opposed to arbitrariness.
- Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India — State action must be just, fair, and reasonable.

### Conclusion

Good but needs cleaner phrasing.

Refined version:

> Therefore, Article 14 ensures that State action remains fair, reasonable, and non-arbitrary. It does not prohibit all classification but permits reasonable classification based on intelligible differentia and rational nexus. Thus, Article 14 is a constitutional guarantee against unequal, irrational, and arbitrary State action.

## Improved Exam Skeleton

1. **Introduction:** Article 14 guarantees equality before law and equal protection of laws to any person within India and prohibits unreasonable classification and arbitrariness.
2. **Equality before law:** Negative concept; no person is above law; no special privilege; everyone subject to ordinary law.
3. **Equal protection of laws:** Positive concept; similarly situated persons must be treated alike; reasonable classification allowed.
4. **Reasonable classification:** Article 14 permits reasonable classification but prohibits class legislation.
5. **Two tests:** Intelligible differentia + rational nexus with the object of the law.
6. **Arbitrariness:** State action must be fair, reasonable, non-arbitrary, and based on objective criteria.
7. **Case laws:** Budhan Choudhry; Ram Krishna Dalmia; E.P. Royappa; Maneka Gandhi.
8. **Conclusion:** Article 14 ensures equality, fairness, and non-arbitrariness in State action.
