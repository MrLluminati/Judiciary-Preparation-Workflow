# Day 2 — Constitution — Article 13 Drill

## Topic

Article 13 — laws inconsistent with Fundamental Rights.

## Learner Answers and Feedback

### Q1. What is Article 13 mainly about?

Learner answer:

> Article 13 is mainly about implementation of Laws in respect of Fundamental Rights. It defines each Laws should be compliant to Fundamental Rights. If any Law violates FRs, it is void to the extent of inconsistency and any new law will be void to the extent of contravention.

Feedback:

Good answer. The learner has correctly captured that laws must conform to Fundamental Rights and that inconsistent/contravening laws become void to the relevant extent.

Refined exam answer:

> Article 13 gives constitutional supremacy to Fundamental Rights by declaring that pre-Constitution laws inconsistent with Fundamental Rights and post-Constitution laws taking away or abridging Fundamental Rights are void to the extent of inconsistency or contravention.

### Q2. What is the difference between Article 13(1) and Article 13(2)?

Learner answer:

> Art. 13(1) makes any old law compliant to the FRs, if any law is inconsistent in respect of FRs, it shall be void. Art. 13(2) keeps check on any new law, it explains that before making any law, it should be checked if that Law doesn't violate the FRs, if it does, then the same shall be void.

Feedback:

Correct distinction. Improve wording: Article 13(1) does not “make the old law compliant”; rather, it makes the inconsistent portion void or unenforceable. Article 13(2) prohibits the State from making laws that take away or abridge FRs.

Refined exam answer:

> Article 13(1) applies to pre-Constitution laws and makes them void to the extent they are inconsistent with Fundamental Rights. Article 13(2) applies to post-Constitution laws and prohibits the State from making any law which takes away or abridges Fundamental Rights; such law is void to the extent of contravention.

### Q3. Does “void to the extent” always mean the whole law is invalid?

Learner answer:

> No, "void to the extent" doesn't always mean the whole law is invalid. It means, laws/part of laws that are inconsistent with respect to FRs are void, the remaining is valid if it can survive on its own.

Feedback:

Excellent answer. This captures the doctrine of severability in simple language.

Refined exam answer:

> No. “Void to the extent” means only the unconstitutional portion becomes void. The valid portion may survive if it is separable and capable of operating independently.

### Q4. Give one simple example of a law that may violate Fundamental Rights.

Learner answer:

> A law stating that the Schools shall permit only Males for education. This violates the Fundamental Right of Education for other genders.

Feedback:

Good instinct. The example correctly identifies exclusion from education as constitutionally problematic. Refine the legal framing: such a law may violate Article 14 equality, Article 15 non-discrimination, and Article 21A/right to education for children in the relevant age group. If the school is State or State-controlled, Article 13/Part III analysis becomes direct.

Refined example:

> A State law providing that government schools shall admit only male students would be unconstitutional because it discriminates on the ground of sex and violates equality and education-related constitutional protections.

## Error / Refinement Points

1. Avoid saying Article 13 “makes old law compliant”; instead say it makes the inconsistent portion void.
2. In examples, connect the violating law to exact rights where possible — Article 14, Article 15, Article 21/21A, etc.
3. Use the phrase “void to the extent of inconsistency/contravention” precisely.
