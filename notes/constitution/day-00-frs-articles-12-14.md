## Teacher Correction — Exam Language

### Fundamental Rights

Fundamental Rights are legally enforceable rights guaranteed under Part III of the Constitution of India, Articles 12–35. They protect individuals against State action and preserve liberty, equality, dignity, and democratic freedoms.

Key phrase for exam:

> Fundamental Rights are justiciable rights enforceable through constitutional remedies, especially Articles 32 and 226.

### Why Fundamental

They are called fundamental because:

1. They are essential for individual liberty and democratic governance.
2. They restrict arbitrary State action.
3. They are enforceable in courts.
4. They are central to the constitutional scheme.

### Article 12 — State

For Fundamental Rights, “State” includes:

1. Government and Parliament of India.
2. Government and Legislature of each State.
3. Local authorities.
4. Other authorities within India or under the control of the Government of India.

Examples:

- Union Government;
- State Government of Bihar;
- Bihar Legislature;
- Municipality;
- Panchayat;
- government-controlled authorities or bodies, depending on control and function.

### Article 13 — Inconsistent Laws

Article 13 explains the effect of laws inconsistent with Fundamental Rights.

#### Article 13(1) — Pre-Constitution Laws

Laws existing before the Constitution become void to the extent they are inconsistent with Fundamental Rights.

#### Article 13(2) — Post-Constitution Laws

The State cannot make any law that takes away or abridges Fundamental Rights. If it does, that law is void to the extent of the contravention.

Key phrase:

> Void to the extent of inconsistency / contravention.

### Article 14 — Equality

Article 14 has two expressions:

1. Equality before law.
2. Equal protection of laws.

Meaning:

- No person is above law.
- Like persons must be treated alike.
- State action must not be arbitrary.

Article 14 is not merely about discrimination grounds like caste, sex, religion etc. Those are more specifically dealt with in Articles 15 and 16.

### Reasonable Classification Test

Article 14 permits classification, but not class legislation.

A classification is valid only if:

1. It is based on an intelligible differentia; and
2. The differentia has a rational nexus with the object sought to be achieved by the law.

### Arbitrariness Test

Arbitrary State action violates Article 14 because arbitrariness is the opposite of equality.
