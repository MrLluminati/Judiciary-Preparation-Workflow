# Article 16 — FAQ / Concept Clarifications

## Purpose

This file records important doubts, practical examples, and exam-useful clarifications arising during Article 16 study. It is written in FAQ style so that the points can be revised quickly before prelims, mains, and interview preparation.

---

# Article 16(4A) — Reservation in Promotion

## Q1. Does Article 16(4A) deal with appointment or promotion?

Article 16(4A) deals with **reservation in matters of promotion**, not ordinary initial appointment.

It enables the State to provide reservation in promotion, with consequential seniority, in favour of **Scheduled Castes and Scheduled Tribes** where, in the opinion of the State, they are not adequately represented in services under the State.

### Memory Line

Article 16(4A) = SC/ST promotion reservation with consequential seniority.

---

## Q2. Who is protected under Article 16(4A)?

Article 16(4A) specifically refers to:

1. Scheduled Castes; and
2. Scheduled Tribes.

It is narrower than Article 16(4), because Article 16(4) refers to backward class of citizens generally, whereas Article 16(4A) specifically deals with SC/ST promotion reservation.

---

## Q3. What is consequential seniority?

Consequential seniority means the seniority benefit that follows from a reservation-based promotion.

If an SC/ST employee is promoted earlier under a valid reservation-in-promotion rule, consequential seniority may allow seniority in the promoted post/cadre to be counted from the date of such promotion.

### Example

A and B are both officers under the State.

- A belongs to the General category.
- B belongs to SC/ST.
- A was senior to B in the lower post.
- A reserved promotion vacancy arises.
- B is promoted earlier through a reserved roster point.
- A is promoted to the same higher post one year later.

If consequential seniority applies, B may be treated as senior to A in the higher/promoted post because B entered the promoted cadre earlier.

### Memory Line

Promotion = movement to a higher post.

Consequential seniority = seniority benefit flowing from that promotion.

---

## Q4. If a General candidate and an SC/ST candidate join on the same date, who gets promoted when only one promotion seat is available?

The SC/ST candidate does not automatically get promoted merely because of caste/category.

The answer depends on:

1. whether reservation in promotion exists under valid service rules;
2. whether the vacancy falls on an SC/ST reserved roster point;
3. whether the SC/ST candidate is eligible;
4. seniority list and promotion criteria;
5. APAR/ACR, integrity, suitability, or departmental criteria;
6. applicable roster and service rules.

If the vacancy is an SC/ST reserved roster point and the SC/ST officer is eligible, the SC/ST officer may be considered/promoted against the reserved vacancy.

If the vacancy is unreserved, all eligible officers compete according to ordinary promotion rules.

---

## Q5. What is a promotional roster?

A promotional roster is a vacancy-allocation register used to identify whether a promotion vacancy is:

1. unreserved;
2. SC reserved;
3. ST reserved; or
4. otherwise reserved, where validly applicable.

It tells the department which promotion vacancy belongs to which category according to the applicable reservation policy.

It does not mean automatic promotion.

A promotional roster operates only when:

1. a valid reservation-in-promotion rule exists;
2. a promotion vacancy arises;
3. the vacancy falls on a reserved roster point;
4. an eligible SC/ST candidate is available;
5. constitutional and service-law requirements are satisfied.

---

## Q6. How can promotional roster be understood in judicial services?

Example: promotion from **Civil Judge Junior Division** to **Civil Judge Senior Division**.

Suppose a promotional roster provides that a particular promotion vacancy is reserved for SC/ST. If that vacancy arises and an eligible SC/ST judicial officer is available, that officer may be considered against the reserved roster point.

If the vacancy is unreserved, all eligible judicial officers compete according to ordinary promotion rules such as seniority, merit, suitability, APAR/ACR, integrity, and service record.

### Important Caution

Reservation in promotion should be understood cadre-wise. Single-post situations are constitutionally sensitive because reservation of a single isolated post may amount to 100% reservation.

---

## Q7. What criteria must the State consider before applying reservation in promotion?

Before providing reservation in promotion, the State should examine:

1. SC/ST representation in the promoted cadre;
2. cadre-wise quantifiable data;
3. total sanctioned posts and working posts;
4. representation in feeder cadre and promoted cadre;
5. vacancy position;
6. eligibility conditions and seniority list;
7. APAR/ACR, integrity, suitability, and performance requirements;
8. administrative efficiency under Article 335;
9. proper roster operation;
10. constitutional limitations under Supreme Court judgments.

### Exam-Safe Point

Article 16(4A) is an enabling provision. It permits the State to provide reservation in promotion for SC/ST, but it does not compel automatic promotion.

---

## Q8. What are the important cases connected with Article 16(4A)?

For revision, remember:

1. **Indra Sawhney v. Union of India** — reservation principles and background to later promotion-reservation amendments.
2. **M. Nagaraj v. Union of India** — reservation in promotion, quantifiable data, inadequate representation, and administrative efficiency.
3. **Jarnail Singh v. Lachhmi Narain Gupta** — no need to prove backwardness of SC/ST again, but inadequate representation and administrative efficiency remain relevant.
4. **B.K. Pavitra cases** — consequential seniority and promotion-reservation issues.

# Article 16(4B) — Carry-Forward Vacancies

## Q9. What does Article 16(4B) deal with?

Article 16(4B) deals with **unfilled reserved vacancies** of a year.

These unfilled reserved vacancies may be treated as a separate class of vacancies and filled in a succeeding year or years.

### Memory Line

Article 16(4B) = carry-forward of unfilled reserved vacancies.

---

## Q10. What are carry-forward vacancies?

Carry-forward vacancies are reserved vacancies that could not be filled in the year in which they arose and are moved to a later recruitment/promotion year.

Reasons may include:

1. eligible reserved-category candidate was unavailable;
2. recruitment was delayed;
3. selection process failed;
4. administrative reasons prevented filling the vacancy.

---

## Q11. Are carried-forward vacancies counted with current year vacancies for the 50% ceiling?

No.

Article 16(4B) permits carry-forward vacancies to be treated as a **separate class of vacancies**. They are not counted together with the current year’s vacancies for determining the 50% ceiling for that year.

### Example

In 2025:

- total vacancies = 10;
- reserved vacancies = 4;
- 2 reserved vacancies remain unfilled.

Those 2 vacancies may be carried forward.

In 2026:

- fresh vacancies = 10;
- current year reserved vacancies = 4;
- carried-forward vacancies from 2025 = 2.

The 2 carried-forward vacancies are treated separately and are not merged with the fresh vacancies for calculating the 50% ceiling.

---

## Q12. Is Article 16(4B) connected with Article 16(4), Article 16(4A), or both?

Article 16(4B) is connected with **both Article 16(4) and Article 16(4A)**.

This is because Article 16(4B) expressly refers to unfilled vacancies reserved under:

1. Article 16(4) — reservation of appointments or posts for backward class of citizens; and
2. Article 16(4A) — reservation in promotion for SC/ST.

### Memory Line

Article 16(4B) = unfilled reserved vacancies under Article 16(4) or Article 16(4A) may be carried forward.

---

## Q13. Does Article 16(4B) mention a fixed upper numerical limit for carry-forward vacancies?

No.

Article 16(4B) itself does not prescribe a fixed numerical upper limit such as one year, two years, or a fixed number of vacancies.

It uses the phrase **succeeding year or years**.

However, carry-forward is not unlimited or arbitrary. It remains controlled by:

1. applicable service rules;
2. reservation roster rules;
3. availability of eligible candidates;
4. validity of reservation under Article 16(4) or 16(4A);
5. Article 14 non-arbitrariness;
6. Article 335 administrative efficiency;
7. judicial review.

### Memory Line

No fixed upper limit in Article 16(4B), but carry-forward is not arbitrary or uncontrolled.

---

## Q14. How can Article 16(4B) operate in judicial services?

Example: promotion from **Civil Judge Junior Division** to **Civil Judge Senior Division**.

If an SC/ST reserved promotion vacancy remains unfilled in 2025 because no eligible SC/ST judicial officer is available, that vacancy may be carried forward to a succeeding year.

When an eligible SC/ST officer becomes available, the carried-forward vacancy may be filled as a separate backlog/carry-forward vacancy, subject to the applicable roster, service rules, eligibility requirements, and constitutional safeguards.

---

# Article 16(5) — Religious / Denominational Institution Exception

## Q15. What does Article 16(5) protect?

Article 16(5) protects laws which require that the holder of an office connected with the affairs of a religious or denominational institution must belong to a particular religion or denomination.

It is an exception to the general non-discrimination rule under Article 16.

### Memory Line

Article 16(5) = religious or denominational office exception.

---

## Q16. Is Article 16(5) applicable to every vacancy in a religious institution?

No.

Article 16(5) should be understood narrowly. It protects offices connected with the religious or denominational affairs of the institution. It does not automatically cover every ordinary administrative or secular job in a religious institution.

### Example

A law may require that the priest or archaka of a Hindu temple must profess the Hindu religion or belong to a relevant denomination connected with that temple.

But an ordinary secular post, such as a clerical or administrative job, may not automatically get the same protection merely because the institution is religious.

---

## Q17. Article 16(5) is an exception to which general rule?

Article 16(5) is an exception to the general rule of equality of opportunity and non-discrimination in public employment, especially non-discrimination on the ground of religion.

### Exam-Safe Point

Article 16(5) does not destroy Article 16. It creates a limited religious/denominational exception for offices connected with the affairs of such institutions.

---

# Article 16(6) — EWS Reservation in Public Employment

## Q18. What does Article 16(6) deal with?

Article 16(6) deals with reservation in appointments or posts in favour of **Economically Weaker Sections of citizens**, other than the classes covered under Article 16(4).

It relates to **public employment**, not educational institutions.

### Memory Line

Article 16(6) = EWS reservation in public employment up to 10%, excluding Article 16(4) classes.

---

## Q19. What is the difference between Article 15(6) and Article 16(6)?

Article 15(6) deals with EWS reservation in educational institutions.

Article 16(6) deals with EWS reservation in public employment, meaning appointments or posts under the State.

### Memory Line

Article 15(6) = EWS in education.

Article 16(6) = EWS in public employment.

---

## Q20. What is the maximum percentage of EWS reservation under Article 16(6)?

Article 16(6) permits reservation for EWS up to **10%** in appointments or posts, in addition to existing reservation.

This 10% reservation is for economically weaker citizens other than those covered under Article 16(4).

---

## Q21. What is Economically Weaker Section?

Economically Weaker Section refers to economically weaker citizens who are not covered by existing reservation categories under Article 16(4), such as backward classes covered by that provision.

For practical recruitment purposes, the detailed EWS criteria are prescribed by government rules or notifications. Commonly, the Central framework uses family income and asset criteria, including gross family income below the prescribed limit and prescribed property/asset restrictions.

### Exam-Safe Point

The Constitution permits EWS reservation through Article 16(6), but detailed income, asset, family, certificate, and authority criteria are prescribed through rules/notifications.

---

## Q22. How is EWS status proved?

EWS status is usually proved through an **Income and Asset Certificate** issued by the competent authority in the prescribed format.

It is not based on mere self-declaration.

The certificate is normally issued by notified revenue or administrative authorities such as DM/ADM/SDM/Tehsildar or equivalent competent authority, depending on the applicable rules and recruitment notification.

---

## Q23. Is an EWS Income and Asset Certificate permanent like a date of birth certificate?

No.

EWS certificate is not permanent like date of birth. It is based on the candidate's family income and assets for the relevant period.

Since income and assets may change, EWS status is generally checked with reference to the financial year or cut-off period required by the recruitment notification.

### Memory Line

EWS certificate = economic-status certificate, not permanent-status certificate.

---

# Article 16 — Whole Article Memory

## Q24. What is the correct one-line memory for Article 16 as a whole?

Article 16 guarantees equality of opportunity for citizens in public employment and permits constitutionally recognised exceptions for residence requirements, reservation, promotion reservation, carry-forward vacancies, religious offices, and EWS reservation.

---

# Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. Do not say Article 16(1) applies only to inadequately represented citizens. It applies to all citizens.
2. Do not call Article 16(3) a reservation clause. It is about residence requirement, and only Parliament can make such law.
3. Do not say Article 16(4A) applies to all backward classes. It specifically refers to SC/ST.
4. Do not say Article 16(4A) gives automatic promotion. It is an enabling provision.
5. Do not confuse promotion with consequential seniority.
6. Do not say Article 16(4B) connects only with Article 16(4). It connects with both Article 16(4) and 16(4A).
7. Do not say Article 16(4B) deals with unreserved vacancies. It deals with unfilled reserved vacancies.
8. Do not say Article 16(4B) itself fixes a numerical upper limit. It does not.
9. Do not treat Article 16(5) as covering every ordinary job in a religious institution. It concerns offices connected with religious/denominational affairs.
10. Do not treat EWS certificate as permanent like date of birth. It depends on income/assets for the relevant period.
11. Do not merge academic backlog with documentation backlog.

---

# One-Page Revision Lines

1. Article 16(1) = equality of opportunity for all citizens in public employment.
2. Article 16(2) = no discrimination in public employment on religion, race, caste, sex, descent, place of birth, residence or any of them.
3. Article 16(3) = Parliament may prescribe residence requirement for certain employment.
4. Article 16(4) = reservation in appointments/posts for backward class of citizens not adequately represented in State services.
5. Article 16(4A) = SC/ST promotion reservation with consequential seniority.
6. Consequential seniority = seniority benefit flowing from reservation-based promotion.
7. Promotional roster = vacancy-allocation mechanism for promotion vacancies.
8. Article 16(4B) = carry-forward of unfilled reserved vacancies under Article 16(4) or 16(4A).
9. Article 16(5) = religious/denominational office exception.
10. Article 16(6) = EWS reservation in public employment up to 10%, excluding Article 16(4) classes.
11. Article 16 as a whole = equality of opportunity in public employment with constitutionally recognised exceptions.
