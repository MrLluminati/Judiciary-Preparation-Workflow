# Article 12 — Definition of State

## Priority

**Priority 1 — Must master.**

Article 12 is a gateway provision for Fundamental Rights because it determines against whom Fundamental Rights can generally be enforced.

## Bare Act Idea

For Part III of the Constitution, unless the context otherwise requires, “State” includes:

1. Government and Parliament of India;
2. Government and Legislature of each State;
3. all local authorities within the territory of India;
4. all local authorities under the control of the Government of India;
5. other authorities within the territory of India or under the control of the Government of India.

## Meaning

“State” under Article 12 is not limited to State Governments. It includes Union authorities, State authorities, local bodies, and other authorities having sufficient governmental character, control, or public function.

## Why Article 12 Matters

Most Fundamental Rights are enforceable against the State. Therefore, before alleging violation of Fundamental Rights, the first issue is whether the respondent body is covered under Article 12.

## Categories of State under Article 12

### 1. Government and Parliament of India

Includes the Union executive and Parliament.

Examples:

- Central Government;
- Union Ministries;
- Parliament of India.

### 2. Government and Legislature of each State

Includes State executive and State legislature.

Examples:

- Government of Bihar;
- Bihar Legislative Assembly;
- Bihar Legislative Council.

### 3. Local Authorities

Includes authorities responsible for local governance.

Examples:

- Municipal Corporation;
- Municipality;
- Panchayat;
- District Board.

### 4. Other Authorities

This is the most important and litigation-heavy part of Article 12.

“Other authorities” may include statutory bodies, corporations, agencies, instrumentalities, or bodies under deep and pervasive governmental control or performing public functions.

## Tests for Other Authorities

Courts may consider:

1. whether the body is created by statute;
2. whether the body is substantially funded by the government;
3. whether the government has deep and pervasive control;
4. whether the body performs public or governmental functions;
5. whether the body enjoys monopoly or special status conferred by the State.

No single factor is conclusive. The totality of control, function, funding, and statutory character is considered.

## Important Case Laws

### Rajasthan State Electricity Board v. Mohan Lal

The Supreme Court gave a broad meaning to “other authorities” and held that statutory bodies exercising public functions may fall within Article 12.

### Sukhdev Singh v. Bhagatram

Statutory corporations such as ONGC, LIC, and IFC were treated as authorities under Article 12 because they were created by statutes and performed public functions under governmental control.

### R.D. Shetty v. International Airport Authority of India

The Court developed the instrumentality or agency test. A body may be State if it acts as an instrumentality or agency of the government.

### Ajay Hasia v. Khalid Mujib

The Court clarified that the form of the body is not decisive. Even a society or corporation may be State if it is financially, functionally, and administratively dominated by government control.

### Pradeep Kumar Biswas v. Indian Institute of Chemical Biology

The Court emphasized the test of deep and pervasive control. Mere regulatory control is not enough; the control must show that the body is financially, functionally, and administratively dominated by the government.

### Zee Telefilms Ltd. v. Union of India

The BCCI was held not to be State under Article 12 because the government did not exercise deep and pervasive control over it. However, bodies performing public duties may still be amenable to writ jurisdiction under Article 226 in appropriate cases.

## Examples

### Example 1

A municipality demolishes property without notice.

It is covered by Article 12 because a municipality is a local authority.

### Example 2

A statutory corporation dismisses an employee arbitrarily.

It may be State if it is created by statute, government controlled, and performs public functions.

### Example 3

A private shop refuses to sell goods to a customer.

Ordinarily, it is not State under Article 12 because a private shop is not a government body or instrumentality.

## Exam Answer Skeleton

Question: Explain the meaning of “State” under Article 12.

Answer structure:

1. Article 12 defines State for Part III.
2. It is an inclusive definition.
3. It includes Union, States, local authorities, and other authorities.
4. “Other authorities” includes bodies acting as instrumentalities or agencies of the State.
5. Mention tests: funding, control, statutory character, public function.
6. Mention cases: R.D. Shetty, Ajay Hasia, Pradeep Kumar Biswas.
7. Conclude that Article 12 is interpreted broadly to prevent the State from escaping constitutional obligations through agencies.

## One-Line Revision

Article 12 defines “State” broadly for Part III and covers governments, legislatures, local authorities, and other government-controlled/public authorities against whom Fundamental Rights may be enforced.

# Article 13 — Laws Inconsistent with Fundamental Rights

## Priority

**Priority 1 — Must master.**

Article 13 gives constitutional supremacy to Fundamental Rights by declaring laws inconsistent with Fundamental Rights void to the extent of inconsistency or contravention.

## Bare Act Idea

Article 13 mainly deals with the effect of ordinary laws that violate Fundamental Rights.

## Article 13(1) — Pre-Constitution Laws

Article 13(1) applies to laws that existed before the commencement of the Constitution.

Rule:

Pre-Constitution laws that are inconsistent with Fundamental Rights become void to the extent of such inconsistency.

## Article 13(2) — Post-Constitution Laws

Article 13(2) applies to laws made after the Constitution came into force.

Rule:

The State shall not make any law that takes away or abridges Fundamental Rights. Any such law is void to the extent of contravention.

## Difference Between Article 13(1) and Article 13(2)

| Basis | Article 13(1) | Article 13(2) |
|---|---|---|
| Applies to | Pre-Constitution laws | Post-Constitution laws |
| Effect | Inconsistent portion becomes void | Law taking away/abridging FRs becomes void |
| Key phrase | Void to the extent of inconsistency | Void to the extent of contravention |
| Purpose | Tests old laws against FRs | Prevents new laws from violating FRs |

## Meaning of “Void to the Extent”

“Void to the extent” does not always mean that the entire law becomes invalid. Only the unconstitutional part becomes void. The rest of the law may survive if it can operate independently.

This is linked to the doctrine of severability.

## Doctrine of Severability

If the valid and invalid parts of a law can be separated, only the invalid part is struck down and the valid part continues to operate.

If the valid and invalid parts are so connected that they cannot be separated, the whole law may fail.

## Doctrine of Eclipse

This doctrine mainly applies to pre-Constitution laws. A pre-Constitution law inconsistent with Fundamental Rights is not completely dead; it is overshadowed or eclipsed by the Fundamental Right. If the constitutional bar is removed later, the law may revive.

## Important Case Laws

### Keshavan Madhava Menon v. State of Bombay

Article 13(1) is prospective in operation. Pre-Constitution laws inconsistent with Fundamental Rights become void only after the commencement of the Constitution and only to the extent of inconsistency.

### State of Bombay v. F.N. Balsara

The Supreme Court applied the doctrine of severability and held that if the unconstitutional parts of a statute can be separated from the valid parts, only the invalid portions need be struck down.

### Bhikaji Narain Dhakras v. State of Madhya Pradesh

The Court applied the doctrine of eclipse. A pre-Constitution law inconsistent with Fundamental Rights becomes dormant or eclipsed but is not wiped out completely.

### Deep Chand v. State of Uttar Pradesh

A post-Constitution law violating Fundamental Rights is void from its inception to the extent of contravention.

### I.C. Golaknath v. State of Punjab

The Supreme Court held that constitutional amendments were “law” under Article 13, but this position was later changed by the 24th Constitutional Amendment and the Kesavananda Bharati case.

### Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala

The Parliament may amend Fundamental Rights, but it cannot alter the basic structure of the Constitution.

## Examples

### Example 1

An old law says members of a particular caste cannot enter public institutions.

After the Constitution, such a law becomes void to the extent it violates equality and non-discrimination.

### Example 2

A new State law says only men can be admitted to government schools.

Such a law may violate Articles 14, 15, and education-related protections, and would be void to the extent of contravention.

### Example 3

A statute contains 20 provisions. Only 2 provisions violate Fundamental Rights and the remaining provisions can operate independently.

Only the invalid provisions may be struck down. The rest may survive under the doctrine of severability.

## Exam Answer Skeleton

Question: Explain Article 13 of the Constitution.

Answer structure:

1. Article 13 protects Fundamental Rights from inconsistent laws.
2. Article 13(1) deals with pre-Constitution laws.
3. Article 13(2) deals with post-Constitution laws.
4. Explain “void to the extent”.
5. Mention severability and eclipse briefly.
6. Cite cases: F.N. Balsara, Bhikaji Narain, Deep Chand.
7. Conclude that Article 13 ensures constitutional supremacy of Fundamental Rights.

## One-Line Revision

Article 13 makes laws inconsistent with Fundamental Rights void to the extent of inconsistency or contravention and thereby preserves the supremacy of Part III.

# Article 14 — Equality Before Law and Equal Protection of Laws

## Priority

**Priority 1 — Must master.**

Article 14 is one of the most important provisions in the Constitution. It prohibits arbitrary State action and unreasonable classification.

## Bare Act Idea

Article 14 says that the State shall not deny to any person:

1. equality before the law; or
2. equal protection of the laws;

within the territory of India.

## Important Word: Person

Article 14 applies to **any person**, not only citizens. Therefore, citizens, non-citizens, and legal persons may claim Article 14 protection where applicable.

## Equality Before Law

Equality before law is a negative concept.

It means:

- no person is above law;
- there is no special privilege in favour of any person;
- every person is subject to the ordinary law of the land.

## Equal Protection of Laws

Equal protection of laws is a positive concept.

It means:

- persons similarly situated must be treated alike;
- the State must give equal legal protection to persons in similar circumstances;
- reasonable classification is permitted.

## Difference Between the Two Expressions

| Expression | Nature | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| Equality before law | Negative | No special privilege; no one above law |
| Equal protection of laws | Positive | Equal treatment of persons similarly situated |

## Article 14 Does Not Mean Identical Treatment

Article 14 does not require the State to treat everyone identically in all circumstances. It requires equal treatment of equals. The State may classify persons or things if the classification is reasonable.

## Reasonable Classification

Article 14 permits reasonable classification but prohibits class legislation.

A classification is valid if it satisfies two conditions:

1. the classification is based on an intelligible differentia; and
2. the differentia has a rational nexus with the object sought to be achieved by the law.

## Intelligible Differentia

Intelligible differentia means a clear, understandable, and identifiable basis of classification.

In simple words:

Why has one group been separated from another group?

Example:

A law gives free bus passes to persons above 60 years of age. The classification is between persons above 60 and persons below 60. Age is a clear basis of classification, so intelligible differentia exists.

## Rational Nexus

Rational nexus means a logical connection between the basis of classification and the object of the law.

In simple words:

Is the classification connected with the purpose of the law?

Example:

If the object of free bus passes is to support elderly persons, classifying persons above 60 has a rational nexus with the object.

## Class Legislation

Class legislation means arbitrary or unreasonable grouping without a proper basis or logical connection with the law’s object. It is prohibited by Article 14.

## Important Case Laws on Reasonable Classification

### State of West Bengal v. Anwar Ali Sarkar

The Court held that classification must not be arbitrary and must be based on a real and substantial distinction connected with the object of the law.

### Budhan Choudhry v. State of Bihar

The Supreme Court clearly stated the two-fold test of reasonable classification: intelligible differentia and rational nexus with the object sought to be achieved.

### Ram Krishna Dalmia v. Justice S.R. Tendolkar

The Court laid down important principles on permissible classification and held that classification may be founded on different bases such as geographical, occupational, or other distinctions, provided it is reasonable.

## Examples of Reasonable Classification

### Example 1 — Maternity Leave

A law grants maternity leave to women employees.

- Intelligible differentia: women employees who may undergo pregnancy/childbirth are classified separately from other employees.
- Object: protection of maternity, health, dignity, and employment security.
- Rational nexus: pregnancy and childbirth create special biological and welfare needs.

Therefore, classification is generally valid.

### Example 2 — Free Legal Aid

A law gives free legal aid to persons earning below ₹3 lakh per year.

- Intelligible differentia: persons earning below ₹3 lakh are classified separately from persons earning above that threshold.
- Object: access to justice and legal representation for economically weaker persons.
- Rational nexus: income level is connected with ability or inability to afford legal assistance.

Therefore, classification is generally valid.

### Example 3 — Invalid Classification

A law gives scholarships only to persons with blue eyes, while the object is to support poor students.

- Intelligible differentia: blue-eyed persons and others.
- Object: support poor students.
- Rational nexus: no logical connection between blue eyes and poverty.

Therefore, classification is invalid.

## Arbitrariness Test

Article 14 also prohibits arbitrary State action.

Arbitrary means based on whim, personal preference, bias, unguided discretion, or without proper reason.

Arbitrariness means the quality of being unreasonable, unfair, irrational, unguided, or not based on relevant principles.

## Article 14 and Arbitrariness

Article 14 is violated not only by unreasonable classification but also by arbitrary State action. State action must be fair, reasonable, and non-arbitrary.

## Important Case Laws on Arbitrariness

### E.P. Royappa v. State of Tamil Nadu

The Supreme Court held that equality is opposed to arbitrariness. Arbitrariness and equality are sworn enemies.

### Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India

The Court expanded the meaning of fairness under Articles 14 and 21 and held that State action must be just, fair, and reasonable.

### Ajay Hasia v. Khalid Mujib

The Court reiterated that Article 14 strikes at arbitrariness in State action and ensures fairness and equality of treatment.

### Shayara Bano v. Union of India

The Court recognized manifest arbitrariness as a ground for invalidating legislation in appropriate cases.

## Example of Arbitrariness

A municipality grants shop licences to 20 applicants but rejects one eligible applicant without giving any reason.

This is an arbitrariness issue. The municipality is a local authority and therefore State under Article 12. If the applicant fulfilled all conditions, rejection without reason, objective criteria, or fair procedure is arbitrary and may violate Article 14.

## Difference Between Classification and Arbitrariness

| Concept | Main Question |
|---|---|
| Reasonable classification | Is the grouping valid? |
| Arbitrariness test | Is the State action fair, reasonable, and non-arbitrary? |

## Article 14 vs Article 15

Article 14 is a general equality provision and applies to any person. Article 15 is a specific non-discrimination provision and applies to citizens on grounds such as religion, race, caste, sex, place of birth, or any of them.

| Basis | Article 14 | Article 15 |
|---|---|---|
| Nature | General equality | Specific non-discrimination |
| Beneficiary | Any person | Citizen |
| Grounds | Broad equality and arbitrariness | Religion, race, caste, sex, place of birth |
| Classification | Reasonable classification allowed | Certain grounds prohibited, subject to exceptions |
