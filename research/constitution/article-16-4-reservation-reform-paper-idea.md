# Research Paper Idea — Article 16(4) Reservation in Public Employment

## Working Title

**Article 16(4) in 2026: Constitutional Necessity, Data Deficit, or Case for Reform?**

Alternative title:

**Reservation in Public Employment under Article 16(4): Should India Repeal, Retain, or Reform?**

## Origin of Idea

This research idea arose during Week 1 — Day 4 — Study Block 1 of the Constitution module while studying Article 16(4), i.e., reservation in public employment for backward classes not adequately represented in State services.

## Core Research Question

Should Article 16(4) continue in its present form, be repealed, or be reformed into a more data-driven and periodically reviewed constitutional mechanism?

## Tentative Thesis

Article 16(4) should not be repealed outright. However, reservation in public employment must be made more data-driven, periodically reviewed, representation-based, and protected from political permanence. The constitutional test should focus not merely on historical backwardness but also on demonstrable present under-representation supported by transparent empirical data.

## Proposed Position

**Retain Article 16(4), but reform its implementation.**

The paper should avoid a simplistic pro-reservation or anti-reservation approach. The stronger publishable position is that Article 16(4) remains constitutionally relevant as a tool of substantive equality, but its legitimacy in 2026 depends on empirical proof, periodic review, creamy-layer control where applicable, and targeted sub-classification where constitutionally justified.

## Debate Structure

### I. Introduction

Frame Article 16(4) as an enabling provision for substantive equality in public employment. The issue is whether its present implementation remains constitutionally justified in 2026 or requires reform.

### II. Case for Retaining Article 16(4)

Arguments:

1. Historical exclusion continues to affect representation in public employment.
2. Article 16(4) is not anti-merit; it is part of substantive equality.
3. Inadequate representation is the constitutional trigger.
4. Sub-classification can make reservation more targeted.
5. Repeal may weaken constitutional social justice protections.

Important cases:

- Indra Sawhney v. Union of India
- M. Nagaraj v. Union of India
- Jarnail Singh v. Lachhmi Narain Gupta
- State of Punjab v. Davinder Singh

### III. Case for Reform / Sunset Review

Arguments:

1. Reservation should not become permanent without fresh data.
2. Backwardness and inadequate representation must be periodically measured.
3. Creamy-layer exclusion and internal inequality need stricter treatment.
4. Benefits may be captured by better-placed groups within reserved categories.
5. Public employment reservation should be linked to actual representation data, not only political claims.

### IV. Case for Repeal

This side may be included for debate balance but should likely be rejected in conclusion.

Arguments:

1. Reservation may reduce focus on public education, capacity-building and skilling.
2. Economic deprivation cuts across caste lines.
3. Long continuation without periodic audit may create constitutional stagnation.
4. Public employment is shrinking, making reservation less effective as a social justice tool.
5. Better tools may include scholarships, education reform, targeted skilling, and economic support.

Counter-position:

Repeal would be abrupt because Article 16(4) is already conditioned on backwardness and inadequate representation. The better constitutional path is review and reform, not deletion.

### V. EWS Angle

Connect Article 16(4) with Article 16(6) and Janhit Abhiyan v. Union of India.

Research question:

If economic disadvantage is now constitutionally recognised, should Article 16(4) also be restructured to better distinguish caste-based social backwardness, economic disadvantage, and actual representation?

### VI. Data Chapter

The paper should collect and analyse:

1. SC/ST/OBC representation in Central Government services by Group A, B, C, and D.
2. State-wise reservation percentages.
3. Backlog vacancy data.
4. Recruitment-wise reserved category appointments.
5. Socio-economic caste survey data from states.
6. Creamy-layer threshold history.
7. Public sector employment trends.
8. Comparison with educational access and income mobility.

Possible data sources:

- DoPT annual reports;
- Parliament questions and answers;
- NCSC reports;
- NCST reports;
- NCBC reports;
- State caste survey reports;
- Census 2011;
- PLFS employment data;
- Supreme Court judgments;
- State reservation Acts and Rules.

## Final Tentative Conclusion

Article 16(4) should be retained as a constitutional safety valve for substantive equality, but it must not operate as an unreviewed permanent entitlement. Its future legitimacy depends on empirical proof of inadequate representation, periodic review, transparent data, creamy-layer control, and targeted sub-classification where constitutionally justified.
